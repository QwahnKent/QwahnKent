About

I am from Vail, Colorado, a small ski town west of Denver. I grew up loving the outdoors, which inspired me to pursue a B.S. in Biology with a concentration in Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at Cornell University.

Currently, I am a Ph.D. candidate in the Ecology and Evolutionary Biology department at Princeton University. My Ph.D. work focuses on animal behavior, particularly how vocalizations provide information about individual identity and how this information affects cooperation in complex societies.

Recently, I have become interested in wildfires and how they impact both animal and human communities.

Ph.D. Projects

Chapter One

Spatial Patterning

Spatial organization and breeding biology of communally nesting Palmchats (Dulus dominicus). Using ~1,200 georeferenced nests, I applied inhomogeneous Ripley's K/L-functions, random labeling tests, and spatial autoregressive models in R to disentangle environmental heterogeneity from active spacing behavior. In review, Journal of Field Ornithology.

Chapter Two

Vocal Mimicry

Context-dependent use of mimetic and referential alarm calls by a cooperative songbird. A reproducible Python pipeline tests the acoustic fidelity of palmchat mimicry of Ridgway's Hawk vocalizations across three field seasons in the Dominican Republic. In prep, target: PNAS.

Chapter Three

Vocal Signatures

Individual, group, and regional signatures in the Palmchat distress call — examining how identity is encoded across nested levels of a multilevel society. In prep, target: Proceedings of the Royal Society B.

Passion Project

Wildfire & Biodiversity

A geospatial analysis assessing wildfire exposure across the ranges of 20 California endemic reptile and amphibian species. Built end-to-end in ArcGIS Online and Python, it integrates CWHR species range data with CAL FIRE historic fire perimeters (1950–2025), framing results around fire-return-interval departure rather than fire avoidance to reflect fire-adapted ecosystem dynamics.

Aim 1: Percentage of Habitat Burned

The first goal of this project is to quantify the percentage of each endemic reptile and amphibian that has been affected by fires between 1970-2000 and 2020-2025 to compare the effects of increasing fires on herp habitat. I determined that the Greenhorn Mountains Slender Salamander,Batrachoseps altasierrae , has the largest delta burn percentage of 47.95%, meaning this species has seen 47.95% more of its habitat burned in the past 5 years than in the three decades between 1970 and 2000 combined. In fact, the Greenhorn Mountains Slender Salamander has had 58.23% of its habitat burned between 2020 and 2025. Explore the map to see how fires from the past 56 years have affected California's endemic herps.

Aim 2: Endemic Herp Hotspots

By overlaying the ranges of 30 endemic reptile and amphibian species, I determined the location of several herp hotspots that are home to at least seven species of endemics. The first hotspot is located in the South Bay outside of Cupertino and Mountain View. Some highlights at this hotspot are the Santa Cruz Black Salamander, Aneides niger, and the San Francisco Gartersnake, hamnophis sirtalis tetrataenia. The next hotspot is located near Ball Mountain in the Greenhorn Mountain range. Highlights include Kern Canyon Slender Salamander, Batrachoseps simatus, the Sierra Night Lizard , and the previously discussed Greenhorn Mountains Slender Salamander. Finally, the last hotspot is near Dennison Mountain. Explore the map to find out which species you can view here!

Aim 3: Prioritizing Conservation

A species-richness surface via hexagonal tessellation and spatial joins identifies biodiversity hotspots, classified into restoration priorities (recently burned) and fire-regime-evaluation priorities (long-unburned), published as an interactive web map and dashboard. The red indicates portions of the hotspots that have been burned in the past five years, while the dark green portions represent portions of the hotspots that have departed from natural fire regimes and could be the location of controlled burns.
